MATE ROV Project

Background

The MATE-ROV Projetc is a project hosted by MATE international. It is a competition to teach science, technology, engineering, and math (STEM) and prepare students for technical careers. Wentworth IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers) is working on a ROV to compete in the June International competition. I was named the mechanical lead for this project, which meant that I was able to work on the project while leading my peers in order to develop all the mechanical designs for the ROV. This taught me valuable lessons in regards to how to effectively manage people, follow the design process, design for manufacturability, and is currently pushing my manufacturing ability to new levels.

The Brainstorm

The first step of this project was some intense brainstorming when the project first came out. The rules were delayed, which put a strain on our already tight schedule. Being college students, we had all left campus during christmas time, which is about when the rules came out. Without being on campus for the first few weeks, almost no progress was made on brainstorming. Because of this, we had to compress our schedule to start in January. We made an agressive schedule and the Gantt Chart to match, and have done our best to stick to it. The schedule called for 3 weeks of design, 6 of manufacturing, a week and a half for testing, and 10 weeks for all the documentation mate requires us to submit, including creating the video of submission. We also scheduled 5 days into the Gantt Chart for the inevitable bumps in the road.

My Contributions

As the mechanical lead, I was *technically* in charge of the mechanical design of the ROV. I say that lightly, since I would never for a second say that all the mechanical designs were mine. They were a combination of a lot of hard work from a lot of different people. Nonetheless, I firmly believe that I made two valuable of contributions to the team.

One: I was nominated as the mechanical lead. When I was nominated I was adamant about saying that I was there to learn, just like every other student on the team. I choose times for meetings, held meetings, resolved interpersonal disputes among teammates, helped others when they needed help, and spear-headed the design of the general use manipulator.

Two: I was also one of four students who did a lot of administrative work in parallel to design, which mostly included hunting for and filling out grants in order to fund the project, which has grown to be quite expensive. Through the hard work of the 4 of us, the project has the funds it will need to succeed, and the students who made valuable contributions to the team will be able to travel, almost completely free.

Solidworks

The CAD is a crucial step for every engineering project, but one that my team actually ran into some trouble with. One of the hardest parts of engineering challenges like this, in my opinion, is knowing when to stop. An idea can be thought over, designed, redesigned, overdesigned, scrapped and reworked before anything is finalized. When this happens, you start to use valuable time inefficiently. I would say that one of the biggest challenges that we as a team faced was understanding when it was time to start manufacturing the ideas that we had so painstakingly created. Throwing those first chips was the hardest part. Now, we've started manufacturing lots of parts, and the ROV is coming together nicely.

Manufacturing

Manufacturing the ROV has taught me tons that I don't think I would ever get the chance to learn in my classes. While starting in on the manufacturing of the ROV, I learned how to make proper drawings from solidworks models, a skill taught to me in my Engineering Graphics course, and honed through this project. Also, it has taught me about proper fits and tolerances, and design for manufacturablity.
