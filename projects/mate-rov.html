MATE ROV Project

Background

The MATE-ROV Project is hosted by MATE international. It is a competition to teach science, technology, engineering, and math (STEM) and prepare students for technical careers. Wentworth IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers) is working on a ROV to compete in the June International competition. I was named the mechanical lead for this project, which meant that I was able to work on the project while leading my peers in order to develop all the mechanical designs for the ROV. This taught me valuable lessons in regards to how to effectively manage people, follow the design process, design for manufacturability, and is currently pushing my manufacturing ability to new levels.

The Brainstorm

The first step of this project was some intense brainstorming when the project first came out. The rules were delayed, which put a strain on our already tight schedule. Being college students, we had all left campus during christmas time, which is about when the rules came out. Without being on campus for the first few weeks, almost no progress was made on brainstorming. Because of this, we had to compress our schedule to start in January. We made an agressive schedule and the Gantt Chart to match, and have done our best to stick to it. The schedule called for 3 weeks of design, 6 of manufacturing, a week and a half for testing, and 10 weeks for all the documentation mate requires us to submit, including creating the video of submission. We also scheduled 5 days into the Gantt Chart for the inevitable bumps in the road.

Solidworks

The CAD is a crucial step for every engineering project, but one that my team actually ran into some trouble with. One of the hardest parts of engineering challenges like this, in my opinion, is knowing when to stop. An idea can be thought over, designed, redesigned, overdesigned, scrapped and reworked before anything is finalized. When this happens, you start to use valuable time inefficiently. I would say that one of the biggest challenges that we as a team faced was understanding when it was time to start manufacturing the ideas that we had so painstakingly created. Throwing those first chips was the hardest part. Now, we've started manufacturing lots of parts, and the ROV is coming together nicely.

My Contributions

As the mechanical lead, I was in charge of the mechanical design of the ROV. I say that lightly, since I would never for a second say that all the mechanical designs were mine. They were a combination of a lot of hard work from a lot of different people. Nonetheless, I firmly believe that I made two valuable contributions to the team.

One: I was nominated as the mechanical lead. When I was nominated I was adamant about saying that I was there to learn, just like every other student on the team. I chose times for meetings, held meetings, resolved interpersonal disputes among teammates, helped others when they needed help, and spear-headed the design of the general use manipulator.

Two: I was also one of four students who did a lot of administrative work in parallel to design, which mostly included hunting for and filling out grant proposals in order to fund the project, which has grown to be quite expensive. Through the hard work of the 4 of us, the project has the funds it will need to succeed, and the students who made valuable contributions to the team will be able to travel, almost completely free.

Manufacturing

Manufacturing the ROV has taught me a lot that I don't think I would ever get the chance to learn in my classes. While starting in on the manufacturing of the ROV, I learned how to make proper drawings from solidworks models, a skill taught to me in my Engineering Graphics course, and honed through this project. Also, it has taught me about proper fits and tolerances, and design for manufacturablity, which has given me a better understanding of my Manufacturing 1000 class.

The ROV was made almost completely manually, something myself and Andrew Zucker, the main machinists, are incredibly proud of. Using only a prototrak mill and lathe each with a DRO, we were able to make some very complex parts, including ones that would be used as o ring surfaces, which requires much tighter tolerances and smoother surface finishes than most can achieve on a manual machine.

Manufacturing for the ROV taught me a lot about proper tolerancing, what tolerancing is "good enough", and the importance of holding tolerance in machining. Multiple failures of the ROV could be attributed to using parts that were not within tolerance. This taught me about what tolerances were most critical and how to note that in a mechanical drawing. Fortunately, almost every tolerance on the ROV was within spec, which allowed it to work incredibly fluidly in water.

Testing

Manufacturing the ROV is coming along nicely, and we are far enough along that we are now able to conduct testing of the main chasis, which included most of the electronics and the drive system, as well as the seals that run along the chasis. A college nearby was gracious enough to let us do some testing in their pool, and we got some interesting results.

We decided that the best course of action was to do a wet test without any electronics to start. That ended up being a very good decision, considering the ROV took on water. After opening it up and draining the water, the leak could have been from a few different places. The most likely source was the seal on the front lense, which dripped out water when removing the ROV from the pool. The other likely source was from non-uniform tightness of the screws in the screw pattern that runs along the O-ring. Without a torque wrench with us at the pool, some screws were found to be too loose, and others were so tight we couldn't get them out without stripping them. The front lense will be resealed before the next round of tests. Also, the stripped screws will be replaced and torqued down uniformly, which will hopefully fix the O-ring seal.

Through some more testing, we were able to slowly find and seal more and more leaks that were being found over the past few weeks. Thanks to the testing in the manufacturing center, we were able to find and seal all the leaks, and the robot is dry and able to do water testing.

Competition

The rules of the conpetition state that teams must enter a submission video in order to qualify for international competition. The video outlined tasks that we had to complete and show to MATE in order to prove our ROV was capable of competing at the international level. The video was due May 11th, and the team spent the month before spending 12+ hours a day in our lab and all over the school, machining, wiring, troubleshooting, testing and hoping we wouldn't break anything. It took us over a week to completely seal the chassis, and over a week to slowly bring all the subsystems online and integrate them into the main chassis. With one week left, we had a few large scale failures in systems that led to sweeping redesigns and late night CAD sessions, early morning machining and testing at noon every day. Thursday the 10th rolled around and we were all nervous for we only had 3 hours total of testing time left at our local pool. Thursday was filled with electrical issues, and we had to push testing until the 11th, the last day. It was clear we would need more pool time than accessible in the city, so we planned to drive an hour and a half west to my hometown in order to use a community pool that was open longer.

The entirety of the night of the 10th was used to solve electrical issues, which ended up being a combination of a short between ESC's and a burnt trace on the sensor board. At 5:30 am, 6 of us left for western mass to spend the day filming. Electrical issues continued to plague us, as well as realizing that precision movement was very hard to achieve with just one driver. We added a second controller to handle some of the degrees of movement, and with that change we were able to more precisely control the ROV. At around 7pm, we filmed a 15 minute, uncut submission video that we hoped was acceptable for MATE. By the time we filmed the rest of the required safety videos and drove home, it was 1 am and we hadn't slept in over 40 hours. But the video was submitted and our almost month long labor of love was ready for competition.
